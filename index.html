We as an industry have spent and continue to spend so much time thinking about, optimizing, and refining our practices. I think it’s worth applying some of the things we’ve discovered to the world outside of software and outside of product development..
I’m going to tell the story how I tried to build up a garden, failed in some ways, and re-tried with more success using some of these practices.

Photo by Growing Places Garden Project

In April 2016, I moved to the Fenway neighborhood in Boston.
If there are some new yorkers in here shuddering at the word ‘Fenway’ or ‘Boston’, don’t worry, I actually grew up in Queens, go Mets.
I moved to this neighborhood, and somehow failed to realize in six years of living in Boston that there is a huge garden community in this park called the Fens.
The Fenway Victory Gardens span about 7 acres of consist of over 500 garden plots that have been in use since 1942.
They were originally WW2 victory gardens to produce local food in a time of war rations. After the war, the Fenway Garden Society was formed, and has since been a volunteer run non-profit.
Upon moving to the neighborhood, I immediately joined the wait list to get a plot.
For as long as I can remember I’ve always wanted a garden and thought urban gardens were the coolest thing. I was really excited, and I was lucky and got one right away. I’ve heard of people waiting for years on this list. It...

Imagery via Google Maps

was a fixer upper though. It had been unmaintained and it was overgrown. I had a ton of work ahead of me to make this plot into the urban oasis I was dreaming of..
That first season, I didn’t know where to begin. I had a general idea to have this nice garden but no concrete vision or goals - it was like I didn’t have any product direction.
I would think of something Big to do, lose focus, and leave projects unfinished. I wasn’t delivering any value to the user (which was me). My garden wasn’t improving at a rate that I felt good about, and I was feeling unaccomplaished by not finishing projects and achieving small wins.
I was clearly lacking in the project management department. For example, at one point I decided that I wanted an herb garden and a brick lined path. I’d go back and forth between the two, and I didn’t prioritize tasks between those projects. I ended up with a half finished path and a weedy herb garden.

Imagery via Total Body Workout

I did learn some valuable lessons that first season, though. ...Like mulching (I didn’t know the magic of mulch?? It would have helped manage some of the weed growth)
THE most valuable, though, was collaborating with my fellow gardeners. I enjoyed meeting people in my row, and loved getting to know a diverse group of people, spanning many different ages, ethnicities, and occupations.
As gardeners, we have mandated volunteer hours to work on the garden as a whole, things like litter clean up, weeding public spaces, and tending our compost piles. I would meet other folks and learn a lot of useful, random tidbits - like how someone used red onions to repel aphids from tomatoes, or the importance of well-drained soil for certain plants.
A valuable piece of advice my plot neighbor gave my partner and me to plant something, anything, so we could have something to grow and enjoy while doing work on the rest of the plot. We planted some tomatoes, and
did end up having a good tomato harvest.
I approached the next season with these lessons from the previous season, and this new lean, agile mentality. I had switched jobs from working on an agile-ish startup engineering team to this current role where I am not only practicing these practices, but I’m constantly teaching them to our clients. I decided to apply some of the principles that were so successful to build software products to building my garden.

Gardening is an inherently iterative process season to season. I was already iterating on what I tried last season, but I wanted to try to use quick iterations and small feedback loops where and if possible. I tried some ideas and was prepared to move on if they didn’t work out

Iteration

One example of this is was starting my own seedlings. Seedlings, if you don’t know, are seeds once they’ve sprouted. They are typically grown inside, like in a greenhouse, with the intention to plant them outside. I bought a dozen or so packets of seeds and peat pods. I was sure the sunny window ledges in my apartment would be a great home for growing seeds. My new cat at the time, Boston,
also thought it was a great home for little sacks of dirt for him to play with and kick around.
...So, That didn’t work out as I had hoped. I ended up buying more seedlings from the store at planting time, but I did have a few that survived. I was happy to have given myself permission to try and fail.
A significant improvement to my strategy was breaking down projects down into tiny, incrememental steps. I’d turn one to-do list item into as many smaller, atomic to-do list items as possible.

Incremental Steps

For example, I decided to build raised beds. I wanted two raised beds - a large, 2x8’ one, and smaller, 2x4’ one. I wrote down “build beds” on my to-do list, but then turned it into as many small items I could.

One task became 14 smaller ones! This was very powerful. It felt good and satisfying to check off individual tasks. It made approaching building this feature of my garden less intimidating, and easiser to plan. It was easier to stop work for the day and know where to pick up the next. It was easisert delegate and parallel process work with my partner.
I mentioned that the most valuable lesson I had learned last summer was the power in my community. I talked to more gardeners in my garden society and got to know more of my neighbors. I learned someone in my row is a rose expert, and spent twenty years volunteering in the rose garden across the park. I took advantage of events for the community, like a seed swap we had. I also talked about my garden a lot. I talked about the sucesses AND failures I was having. I talked about gardening to my coworkers and learned we have quite the group of gardeners at work. I’m talking about it now.

Collaboration

One hot tip I learned from a coworker was to use fox urine to repel rabbits. The fens has a huge rabbit problem. It’s ironic because I’ve heard it’s an endangered species somewhere so we can’t do much about them. But, one of my coworkers was also facing a rabbit problem and told me how you can go to the garden store and buy a bottle of fox urine! It saved my portugese kale.

Imagery via Pioneer Press: Scott Takushi

Through iterating on ideas and plans, working incrementally, and collaborating with my community, things we talk about in tech all the time, building my garden was much more fun and productive process. We spend so much time as people who build software refining and optimizing our processes. They don’t always translate outside of software project management, but I really believe it’s worth exploring how to apply some of these great ideas in other areas. At the heart of this, we want to satisfy us, our users.
